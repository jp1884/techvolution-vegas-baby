## Chapter 2—Booklet: The Rules to the Game

You pick up the booklet and flip to its first page.

It starts off with "Imagine you are a person in a perfectly flat world. A land where everything is two-dimensional." As you continue reading you learn a 2D existence is like an avatar living on a computer screen. You can move left, right, up, or down, but never can you jump out from the screen.

Because in this world, there's no height.

In this 2D reality, avatars are like magnets on a fridge, bumper cars on a track, or cookie cutters on a baker's table. Only able to move two ways, a person would have to feel an object to know about it. Every person and thing is just lines of various polygons.

Four line and angles make a square, five a pentagon, six a hexagon, and so forth into great variety. To these creatures, 3D entities would appear as merely passing through their 2D world, appearing first as a point, then growing into a weird curved line, then shrinking back to a point again.

This booklet takes this idea from "Flatland: A Romance of Many Dimensions". Written by Edwin A. Abbott, that book popularized dimensional thinking and is often used in mathematics, philosophy, and science education like this booklet to get people to think of totally alien environments.

By bringing us into a 2D world, Abbott makes us feel lost. Learning about Flatland opens the door for us to see our reality differently and thereby better question and understand our own existence.

We sometimes experience this feeling of being lost. Once, I wanted to enjoy some back country open roads. I thought I was going north and would inevitably hit the highway. Driving alone, windows down, with no phone coverage, and no maps or compass, I had a blast. I was actually going east and hit nothing. Standing outside the beater car with little gas was quite disorienting and somewhat scary as night fell.

Many will have more dangerous stories, others more comical, but we all should remember that feeling of being lost. It marks an error between our expectations and reality. As we forge a new technological identity, we must embrace this feeling for it guides us to investigate, instead of fear, what we find in the dark.

Abbott wrote his famous story to open new doors of awareness. We need it because our perceptions are nothing but our brain interpreting what our senses signal when intepreting reality. But, there is so much we can't perceive, from very small things like bacteria, to confusing things like how atoms act. In fact, almost all of reality is outside our natural senses. Like a hexagon in Flatland, we must feel through the darkness with what we have.

Note, it's not a choice. The Square stuck in the 2D world is not stupid or lazy. He reasons carefully in every dimension he can perceive, while his fate is decided in one he can't. If you remember Maria, this is exactly her problem. Every identity label she had, Californian, graduate, regional manager etc. was a dimension she could see. None of them said "sells physical media in the Internet Age".

Thankfully, we've long used our senses and minds to investigate nature and piece together a pretty good picture of it in fields of science.

Physics and chemistry describe our reality, and constrain how geology and biology work. Scientists often joke that chemistry is "just" applied physics and biology is "just" applied chemistry. Although this always seems to get a laugh, everyone knows each level's phenomena aren't obvious from the level below. Which is why they all get to keep their jobs (even the geologists, who always seem to get the worst of this joke).

The point is, although together these fields test, record, study the universal laws of nature, unlike chess or Monopoly, there is no actual booklet to read or program to hack. We perceive and describe these laws as best we can, but nature's rulebook never cares about our input, ideas, or feedback.

The laws of nature can be hard to communicate. For example, when huge waves move across an ocean, that is the Sun's radition heating up an area, causing air to warm, rise, and move to cooler areas. This wind rushes over water, bringing waves. However, to a sailor this phenomena is an "angry sea". The point is, to themselves the laws are abstract. Relative to life, the laws are personal. They bring dangers, opportunities, life must navigate. 

And this is what life learns to decode among the singals received.

> I'll write throughout this book that life wants things. Plants want water. Or humans want companionship. I don't mean lifeforms reviews and selects from a menu of options and wants "ice water". That want is a desire, a rare pleasure. When I say life wants something I mean to say, it has no choice but to seek hydration, eat calories, avoid rough seas. While simple terms do not convey the laws of natures deepest secrets, only using abstract names can be like not understanding the rules at all. Wanting this shelter opporunity or avoiding this water danger is what keeps the lifeforms alive, and is a defining feature.

Lifeforms learn to spot dangerous signals, and therefore wants to flee. Wants are the simplified names for requirements. While sailors meteorists may discuss biometric pressure, a sailor may think "rough seas", and a fish may feel something's off, they are all perceiving the same rulebook and acting to keep themselves going inside it.

Flatland makes it easy ofr us to see these multiple dimesions to reality. This is why life's evolution is such a persistent and universal process. Life's reproduction is the only process that produces variants in enough abundance to test what works in any given environment. Evolution is the result and driver of countless species over billions of years as a result.

Variants are Squares scattering across the board, feeling their way through surroundings they never chose. Which one you're born as is luck. What happens next isn't.

Touching around in the darkness turns our senses—touch, smell, and sight—into crude teachers. Success brings pleasure, failure brings pain, and slowly these experiences guide us to repeat what works and avoid what doesn't. Natural selection will slowly create genotypes that better use water (camels, llamas) and phenotypes that learn where the water holes are (elephants, giraffes). We can enact some of this learning method anytime we stop drinking water.

Feeling true thirst will soon drive anyone to seek water to stop the pain of dehydration.

That method only changes our habits, and over generations of life and death, our biology. After all, even a camel cannot decide to hold more water; it can only be born slightly better or worse at it, ten thousand times over. But we choose our tools. We are the first lifeform whose variations can aim at their needs and evolve solutions thousands of times in a single lifetime.

We can be like avatars jumping off the screen to see our 2D reality from a higher place. Because when we know the rules of the game, we can evolve our new technological identity with purpose.

We need to.

Because streaming had no mind. It found users who wanted a movie tonight without leaving the couch. Not Maria's market, not her job, just the movie. That combination of tool and user wanted to exist, the way a camel wants water. It never consulted Maria, or her employer, or her politics. Nobody wanted her gone. She was gone regardless.

Internet Age technology is changing our existence very fast and in countless ways. We've already discussed streaming. Another example is the Mars rovers. These are technological explorers navigating an alien place. They extend human perception far beyond our biological limitations. Sending back information from another planet we may one day colonize transforms our existence in ways we don't yet appreciate.

Living with technology so powerful we might soon be a multiple planetary species means we're in such a new place we're not unlike the Square trying to understand the three dimensions for the first time.

Consider how, this is a ongoing process. AI is new for example. It has no mind either, but surely wants to exist.

What does that mean for us all today?

With so much technological change today, our best life depends on understanding the rules of the reality we inhabit. Just as a two-dimensional being must accept its constraints to navigate its world, we must recognize the fundamental laws that govern our existence. These rules are all powerful and they do not care about our wants, plans, or beliefs any more than Flatland cares about a triangle's or square's desire to jump.

Our deep acceptance of the rules of the game is the foundation for the mastery we need to move from being biological to technological beings.

Unlike anything else in the universe (that we know of), lifeforms grow, learn, and pass on genes—and sometimes lessons—to their next generations. Humans are only different in our increased perspective. Most creatures depend on instincts and pains to find resources, while we are a little more self-aware of why we need shelter, food, water, and procreation to stay alive. More knowledge is great, because it means more accuracy about what we need and how to get it.

Therefore, unlike Flatland, our planet is not just a place of existence, but a proving ground where the laws of nature determine which variants persist and which do not. Each of us is a unique shape. This book's goal is to help us all see the gameboard more clearly.

So let's jump up off the screen and see what the game board of our three dimensions looks like. In the next chapter, we'll find it's filled with natural laws making a gameboard that emerges through life's encounter with the universe.
