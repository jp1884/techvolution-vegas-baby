This illustration is showing something hard to put into words.

On the left side of the frame: a near-empty cinema. Visit the movie theater on most showtimes and this is what it looks like. A few slumped figures in the seats. Walk in on a Wednesday afternoon and you'll wonder if the projectionist is still employed. I was one, and I often wondered how much longer my job was going to last.

Meanwhile, on the right side: a packed amphitheater. An ocean of faces. Stage lights blazing down through the rafters. Two figures on a stage — one in a tuxedo presenting an award, one accepting it in an evening gown. The crowd is roaring.

Same picture. Same frame. Two rooms. Two existences. One reality.

## The Long Decline

The cinema has been emptying for over sixty years.

In 1960, 18% of Americans went to the movies once a week. By 1964, 10%. By 1972, 8%. Per capita ticket sales were 30 in 1946, 12 in 1955, 5 in 2000, and about 2 today. The industry lives off marketing and hypes up bigger-sounding "gross dollars" instead of the number of tickets sold. News outlets pump up a "200 million weekend gross", and it sounds like an amazing achievement, but that is about 13 million views spread over three days for a population of 380 million, or about 3.5%. Which is definitely not going viral.

Either way you slice it, television kept taking movie seats and never gave them back.

Years ago, the movies introduced the blockbuster system to compensate. *Star Wars*, *Jurassic Park*, sequels, franchise expansion, brand-owned culture. By limiting the stories told, the studios stopped being centers of production and hoped to become owners of the rights to American storytelling.

The 2000s added more spectacle. D-Box seats that shake. 3D, again, like in the 1950s. IMAX premiums. Marketing budgets that sometimes equaled production budgets. By the late 2000s, state tax credits had become a critical source of external funding for the film industry — meaning the business of selling tickets no longer covered the cost of making the movies.

Then streaming arrived, and the public discovered they preferred watching at home with cheaper prices, no ads, and affordable popcorn. COVID — a pandemic Hollywood often makes movies about — cleared many of the remaining regular customers.

The award shows tell a similar story. But you have to look past the lights to see it.

The Oscars peaked in 1998 at over 55 million viewers. Recent ceremonies have pulled around 20 million. The Golden Globes nearly went off the air a few years ago. The headlines from each ceremony focus less on the films and more on the host's monologue, who wore what, and what got said on a microphone that wasn't supposed to be hot.

Meanwhile your streaming queue keeps growing. You watch on a couch. You skip the credits. You increasingly don't pay attention to what is opening this weekend at the box office.

The cinema is often empty because we left. The award shows are gossip fodder because our society has never seen the movies.

We left because the product thinned out, the prices went up, and the alternatives got better.

## So What

A century ago, the cinema was the spectacle. Film was new, exciting, and new releases drew mass crowds because they were the closest thing the public had to a shared event. *Gidget* (1959), a movie you likely never heard of, introduced an obscure sport called surfing to America, driving millions into the sea to try it. *Jaws* (1975) used beach culture to terrorize those taking a swim, and turned extremely rare shark attacks into nearly expected events.

Such was the power of the movies.

Now the shared event is the ceremony about the films — and the films themselves are most often just more ubiquitous content, likely watched alone at home, and so rarely discussed across society because viewers are quickly watching something else.

People will pay for a good night out. Music concerts, wrestling shows, and sports events have all maintained their seat counts. We left the cinema because the product thinned out, the prices went up, and the alternatives got better.

We know this is happening. Do the industry and its stars recognize it openly? And if they did, would they have a framework to understand and manage it?

As movie legend Jim Carrey reflected after one notorious awards show, "We're not the cool kids anymore."

## The Figure at the Curtain

Look at the image once more. There's a fourth element I didn't mention.

He's standing at the curtain that divides the two halves. He's in a suit. He's facing the empty cinema rather than the spotlight. And he's removed something from his face.

I'll explain him in Part 2 — along with what he sees, and why a Hollywood musical from 1952 turns out to be the key to understanding what's happening.

# Why is the cinema empty while the award show is full?

*Part 2 of 2*

*[image goes here]*

He steps away from the curtain.

The mask is in his hand. The crowd behind him is still roaring. The cinema in front of him is still empty. Both rooms are still real.

Two stages. Two existences. One reality.

He looks back at the awards stage. He recognizes everyone up there. The man at the microphone he's known since they were both going to open auditions. The woman accepting the award did endless casting calls, committed her soul to the role she's being celebrated for. The producers in the front row financed three of his favorite films of the last decade.

These are people who made art, the same way generations before them did. Why not enjoy their achievements? Why not have fun?

Then he looks at the cinema. He recognizes those seats too. He sat in them as a boy. He worked in one as a kid handing out popcorn. He has spent his whole career trying to fill them. To spread his art.

Both are happening at the same time. The roar of the room behind him sounds like an industry in good health. The empty seats in front of him say it isn't.

Every usher like him sees the empty seats. Every star can't seem to see past the stage lights.

He can see it on their faces. They're not pretending — they genuinely believe the applause they're hearing means what it used to mean when Hepburn and Bogart graced the silver screen.

The masks of identity — artist, special, star — keep them from noticing. He used to wear it too. For most of his career, he didn't see the empty seats either. He saw the audition room, the camera lens, the red carpet, rooms he was in.

Like the best stories, this awakening has happened before. An industry, an institution, a company — the people inside didn't see what outsiders knew was obvious. Despite data, meetings, and good intentions, the people inside were stuck inside a paradigm and couldn't see the failure from within.

He thinks of an old movie. *Singin' in the Rain*, 1952. The silent movie star Lina Lamont is live on stage, getting raucous cheers, while the unknown talent Kathy Selden sings hidden behind the curtain. The audience celebrates the wrong person because one was a star of a different era of silent movies.

The truth is backstage, hidden, while the spectacle out front receives the applause. The film was a comedy. The transition it was about — silent film to sound — was real, and it ended careers.

The man with the mask doesn't know all the details, but the same story is playing in front of him now. He stands there with the mask in his hand and the question won't leave him alone. Why does this keep happening?

He thinks of a role he played years ago. A prince, in one of the European period pieces. He remembers the amazing sets. The gold leaf on the throne, the gold cutlery, the robed extras following history and obeying his commands. He remembers learning the cathedrals were built — with complete sincerity — for the people to see the grandeur of their society. Heck, peasants could buy tickets to even tour the Palace of Versailles.

And so, no royal thought anything was wrong. The ceremonies ran on schedule. The applause sounded the same as it always had.

He looks at the stage behind him. The costumes look different. The applause sounds the same. Everyone missing the point.

He sets the mask down on a chair near the curtain. He doesn't put it back on. Maybe industries and technologies follow patterns the way biology does. Maybe nobody has been looking for them. He doesn't know. He's just the one standing at the curtain, wondering.
